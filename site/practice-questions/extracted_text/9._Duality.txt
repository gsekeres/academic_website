2.1     Weak duality
Since every dual function is an upper bound, it follows that

                                                        p‚ąó ‚Č§ d‚ąó .

This property, which always holds, is called weak duality. It holds even if d‚ąó and p‚ąó are infinite.
For example, if the primal problem is unbounded above so that p‚ąó = ‚ąě, then d‚ąó = ‚ąě. If the
dual problem is unbounded below so that d‚ąó = ‚ąí‚ąě, then p‚ąó = ‚ąí‚ąě. The latter observation, in
particular, allows us to check the feasibility of p‚ąó .


2.2     Duality gap and strong duality
The difference d‚ąó ‚ąí p‚ąó is referred to as the (optimal) duality gap which must be nonnegative by
weakly duality. Whenever the duality gap is zero, i.e., d‚ąó = p‚ąó , we say that strong duality holds.
Thus, whenever strong duality holds, the upper bound given by the value of the dual problem
is tight. Conditions that ensure strong duality holds are called constraint qualifications and an
example of one is the Slater‚Äôs condition we saw when we studied KKT. To be complete, when both
the objective and constraints are concave, the slater‚Äôs condition holds if there exists an x ‚ąą Rd that
satisfies the constraints,1 and
                                          gj (x) > 0 ‚ąÄj ‚ąą {1, 2, . . . , J} .

For any inequality constraint j ‚ąą {1, 2, . . . , J} for which gj is affine, we can relax the condition
above to be a weak inequality; i.e., gj (x) ‚Č• 0.
